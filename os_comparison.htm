Comparison of Operating Systems

Compared are 15 different operating systems

  • Windows: Most popular desktop OS. High overall market share. Probably the best software and hardware compatibility of all OS, due to its dominant market position. Comes pre-installed on most PCs sold.
  • macOS: Unix. Significant market share and great software support. Comes pre-installed on all Apple Macs.
  • iOS: Unix. The mobile equivalent to macOS, pre-installed on the iPhone. Significant market share.
  • Android: Unix-like, based on Linux. Most popular smartphone OS, pre-installed on almost all smartphones that are not made by Apple. Highest market share of all end-user OS.
  • Chrome OS: Unix-like, based on Linux. Decent market share in certain markets (e.g. US). Preinstalled on Chromebooks.
  • GNU/Linux: Unix-like. Widespread use in the server and embedded markets, still significant as a desktop and smartphone OS. Most popular distributions for desktops include Ubuntu, Debian, OpenSUSE, Fedora, and Arch.
  • FreeBSD: Unix. Without any doubt the most popular of the BSD systems, is used in production or as part of their product by large companies (e.g. Apple, Sony, Netflix). Has desktop-specific distributions such as GhostBSD.
  • NetBSD: Unix. Has been part of various commercial products. General purpose OS that can be used on the desktop, i.e. it runs modern browsers and modern desktop environments.
  • OpenBSD: Unix. Fork of NetBSD. General purpose OS that can be used on the desktop. Has influenced other OS.
  • Illumos: Unix. Open source fork of Solaris 10 that has several distributions ranging from commercial to hobbyist, and target various markets such as cloud, NAS, server, and desktop (e.g. OpenIndiana).
  • Haiku: One of the most promising of the alternative OS. Has a relatively complete selection of software, including a modern browser. Attempts to re-create BeOS, a commercially failed desktop OS of the late 90s/early 2000s.
  • ArcaOS: While it is a legacy OS catering towards remaining users of OS/2, with fundamental limitations (e.g. stuck to 32-bit), Arca Noae has undertaken many significant improvements such as hardware compatibility or the possibility of using more than 4 GB RAM. ArcaOS can also run modern browsers.
  • RISC OS: Legacy OS from the late 80s that was open sourced in 2018. Used to be popular as a desktop OS in the UK. Limited in its design (e.g. no pre-emptive multitasking, no support for multi-core CPUs or 64-bit). Unlikely to ever become "relevant" again besides its legacy user base. Yet, it has recently received a modern web browser (Iris) and was ported to the Raspberry Pi.
  • AmigaOS: Legacy OS from the 1980s with slow development progress. Back in the day it was an impressive OS for home users. Suffers from limited hardware support (32-bit PowerPC only) with no plans to change this. Disappointing browser situation (best browser is Odyssey Web Browser/OWB, which is still receiving updates as of 2022 but is fundamentally based on a 2013 version of Webkit with backports/updates added). Long-term outlook is unclear as it is proprietary, commercial OS.
  • MorphOS: Despite its negligible market share and limited hardware compatibility (PowerPC only, mostly obsolete devices; no SMP or 64-bit support), this appears to be a relatively polished OS and has the most modern web browser (Wayfarer) for all AmigaOS-like operating systems. Good enough to be a daily driver for some.

Also worth mentioning...

There are of course many more operating systems, for an extensive list see here. I have not included the following:

  • OS that have never been marketed towards end users in any way, e.g. z/OS, IBM i, MCP, VSEn, VxWorks and many more.
  • OS that are not developed anymore, e.g. Symbian, BlackBerry OS, BlackBerry 10, OS/2, Mac OS 9, IRIX, MS-DOS, ZETA, Syllable, ...
  • OS that have not had any updates since 2020 even though they are not confirmed to be dead, e.g. UnixWare, Minix, Inferno, PhantomOS, Ghost, ...
  • This still leaves an extensive list of OS that I didn't include but are worth mentioning:
    • Unix
      • AIX: Commercial Unix from IBM. Not marketed towards workstation/desktop use anymore (e.g. Firefox browser and CDE desktop haven't been updated in years). While it will be supported until at least 2029, the long-term outlook for this OS is unclear as IBM is more likely to focus on its competing offer Red Hat Enterprise Linux. AIX doesn't run on commonly available consumer hardware (only POWER ISA).
      • DragonFlyBSD: Would almost look the same as FreeBSD in this comparison (as DragonFlyBSD is a fork of FreeBSD). More suitable as a daily driver than some other niche OS (e.g. you can install the Xfce desktop and Firefox browser on DragonFlyBSD). However, its existence appears to depend on its founder (Matt Dillon) with relatively little developer interest compared to other BSDs, and neglible usage share both on desktops and servers. While an interesting OS, it seems to be more of a research project rather than something that is really used in production (yet).
      • HP-UX: Commercial Unix from HP. Not marketed towards workstation/desktop use anymore (e.g. Firefox browser and CDE desktop haven't been updated in years). Doesn't run on commonly available consumer hardware (only Itanium ISA). End of life announced for 2025 for both Itanium hardware and HP-UX support. It's a dead end.
      • Solaris: Commercial Unix from Oracle. Would almost look the same as Illumos in this comparison (as Illumos is a fork of Solaris). While Solaris was historically used for workstations and still includes relatively up to date desktop software (e.g. modern GNOME desktop and Firefox browser), it is clear that Oracle is focusing on the commercial server market. Even though Solaris is still regularly getting new features and improvements, the long-term perspective has been put into doubt. The current version is still supported until at least 2034, which is generally seen as the end of life date for Solaris. Go on Oracle's website and you'll see that they're focused more on Oracle Linux rather than Solaris. The SPARC ISA, one of the main arguments to buy a Solaris computer, is on its way out as well.
    • Unix-like
      • 9front: While still actively developed, this is a fork of a legacy OS (Plan 9) that never found widespread adoption in the first place. Lack of basic applications expected by most users (such as a modern web browser). Hard to use for novice users due to its unusual interface.
      • QNX: Used to be a fine OS for the desktop, with mainstream browsers and a full desktop environment available. While it still has a modern built-in browser (using the Blink engine), the focus has shifted to embedded and automotive use. The Photon GUI has been completely removed, making it pointless as an end-user OS. BlackBerry 10 OS for smartphones was based on QNX but is also not developed anymore.
      • Redox: Relatively recent project (started 2015) and more of a hobbyist OS, mostly developed by Jeremy Soller. Currently still too immature to be used as a daily driver (e.g. no modern browser available), but has potential to become a serious contender.
      • SerenityOS: Relatively recent project (started 2018) and more of a hobbyist OS, mostly developed by Andreas Kling. Currently still to immature to be used as a daily driver, mostly just runs as a virtual machine at this stage. Interestingly, SerenityOS includes its own browser developed from scratch, which is suprisingly capable of rendering "modern" websites.
      • ToaruOS: Relatively recent project and more of a hobbyist OS, mostly developed by K. Lange. Currently still to immature to be used as a daily driver, mostly just runs as a virtual machine at this stage. Doesn't have a modern browser.
    • Other
      • A2 (formerly Bluebottle): Never found any widespread adoption or real-world use outside of ETH Zürich.
      • AROS: Generally seen as the most immature and unstable of the three AmigaOS-like systems (AmigaOS, AROS, MorphOS). The best available browser is OWB which is fundamentally still based on 2013 Webkit with backports and smaller updates, and might not be fully compatible with "modern" websites. Slow development, few developers. Still, it might survive AmigaOS and MorphOS in the long-term, as AROS runs on x86 hardware and has some other modern features (such as 64-bit support and SMT).
      • FreeDOS: While some laptops are available with FreeDOS preinstalled, this OS is not suitable for the average user, lacking a graphical interface and modern browser. It's just a niche OS for running old DOS software.
      • Fuchsia: Currently only used in niche/embedded devices (e.g. Google Nest). Relatively young OS with unclear roadmap for the future. The graphical user interface (Armadillo) has been removed from Fuchsia. Knowing Google's tendency to kill its projects, it wouldn't be surprising if Fuchsia development is discontinued suddenly.
      • Genode: One of the most promising and quickly progressing alternative operating systems. Can run a modern web browser (Falkon) and supports a variety of hardware, from PC to Pinephone. Sculpt OS is intended to be a distribution for desktop users, however the GUI is not easy to understand for normal users. Once this runs a "normal" desktop I'd like to include it in the comparison.
      • HelenOS: Research OS from Charles University Prague with no known production use.
      • KolibriOS: Hobby OS without any known production use
      • MenuetOS: Hobby OS without any known production use
      • OpenVMS: Not marketed towards workstation/desktop use anymore (e.g. Firefox browser and CDE desktop haven't been updated in years). Long-term outlook for this OS is unclear as HP previously announced its end of life before then selling it to VSI (the current owner). Recently ported to x86, but mostly with a focus on running as a virtual machine. Target customers seem to be users of legacy OpenVMS servers, definitely not focused on end users anymore.
      • ReactOS: Attempts to be a free clone of Windows with full binary compatibility, but is decades behind and unlikely to ever catch-up. Currently not stable enough as a daily driver.
      • Visopsys: Another hobbyist OS. No modern browser, no real use case.

What about market share?

The below diagram shows the historical market shares of operating systems across personal devices (meaning "workstations", "PC", desktops, laptops, tablets and smartphones, but excluding for example servers). At the bottom you will see a short genealogy of the major "consumer" operating systems. Many historical operating systems families are long dead, some other are somehow still alive but barely so or have stopped not targeting the personal computing or workstation market anymore.

In some cases, the old OS name has been kept even though its successor was a completely different product, technologically speaking. This includes Windows (versions up to Win 98 and Win ME based on MS-DOS, versions since Win 2000 based on NT), Blackberry OS (version 10 based on QNX), and macOS (version 10 based on the Unix-like Nextstep).
